Five technical reports came out within weeks of each other this summer: [Kimi K3](https://www.kimi.com/blog/kimi-k3) from Moonshot AI, [GLM-5.2](https://www.zhipuai.cn/zh/research/161) from Zhipu, [MAI-Thinking-1](https://microsoft.ai/pdf/mai-thinking-1.pdf) from Microsoft AI, [DeepSeek-V4](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.19348) from DeepSeek-AI, and [LongCat-2.0](https://longcat.chat/blog/longcat-2.0/) from Meituan. All five are Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) language models. All five make different, specific choices about how routing, load balancing, and expert granularity should work — and none of those choices came from nowhere. This post is about the mechanics of a modern MoE layer, where each of these five reports' design choices actually trace back to, and what's converged versus what's still being actively contested.

A note on sourcing before anything else: the Kimi K3 and DeepSeek-V4 numbers below come directly from their own release material. The MAI-Thinking-1 numbers come from a full read of Microsoft's technical report. GLM-5.2's own announcement page is thin on MoE-specific architecture numbers (it focuses on coding benchmarks and infrastructure), so its expert-count and top-K figures here are compiled from third-party technical coverage rather than Zhipu's own text — treat them as well-sourced but secondary. LongCat-2.0's blog page did not render its architecture details in a form this post's research could directly capture, so its numbers are likewise from third-party coverage. Where a number is secondary-sourced rather than confirmed directly from the model's own report, it's noted as such.

## 1. Anatomy of a modern MoE layer

Strip away everything vendor-specific and a 2026-era MoE layer looks like this: a token arrives, a small router network scores every expert's "affinity" for that token, the top-K highest-scoring experts actually run their feed-forward computation, and — in almost every current design — one additional shared expert runs on every token regardless of what the router decided. The outputs get combined into a single vector, weighted by the router's own scores, and that's the layer's output.

![Anatomy of a modern MoE layer](blogs/images/moe-layer-anatomy.svg?v=1)
^One token, one layer: every routed expert is a candidate, only a handful actually run — the shared expert is the one exception that always fires.

The entire appeal of this design is in the gap between two numbers. **Total parameters** scale with how many experts exist in the pool — this is the number vendors put in a headline ("2.8 trillion parameters"). **Active parameters** scale with how many experts actually run per token — top-K plus the shared expert — and this is the number that actually determines inference cost. A model can have a enormous total parameter count and still be cheap to run per token, provided K stays small relative to the pool. That gap between total and active is the entire reason MoE exists, and every design choice covered in this post is, one way or another, an attempt to widen that gap without hurting quality.

Two mechanical details matter enough to call out here, because every report below makes an explicit choice about them:

**The gating function.** The router needs to turn a token's representation into a score per expert. Early MoE work used softmax across all experts (forcing them to compete for a fixed probability budget); more recent designs use sigmoid, scoring each expert independently, which turns out to matter once you're routing to dozens of experts out of hundreds rather than a handful out of eight.

**Load balancing.** Nothing in a router's training objective naturally prevents it from routing every token to the same three experts and ignoring the rest — a failure mode usually called routing collapse. Every design here has some mechanism to prevent it, and the specific mechanism has changed more over the last two years than almost anything else in this space.

## 2. Where this actually comes from: nine years of lineage

None of the five 2026 reports invented Mixture-of-Experts, and most of what looks novel in any one of them is a variation on a much older idea, or a direct continuation of one specific 2024 paper.

![Nine years of MoE design](blogs/images/moe-lineage-timeline.svg?v=1)
^Most of 2026's frontier MoE reports trace back to one 2024 fork: DeepSeekMoE's fine-grained-experts-plus-shared-expert recipe.

**Shazeer et al., 2017** introduced the modern sparsely-gated MoE layer for deep learning: a noisy top-k softmax router with an auxiliary loss to keep expert usage balanced. This is the ancestor of essentially everything that follows.

**GShard (2020)** scaled the idea to a transformer trained with real expert parallelism across hardware — splitting experts across devices and using all-to-all communication to route tokens to whichever device holds their assigned expert. This is also where the standard load-balancing auxiliary loss formula comes from, and where a capacity factor (a hard cap on how many tokens each expert can accept before overflow tokens get dropped) entered the standard toolkit.

**Switch Transformer and GLaM (2021)** pushed in different directions from the same base: Switch Transformer simplified routing to top-1 (one expert per token) and showed MoE could train stably even at that extreme, while GLaM used top-2 routing to scale to 1.2 trillion parameters while activating only a small fraction of them per token, an early, clean demonstration of decoupling total parameters from compute cost.

**ST-MoE and expert-choice routing (2022)** addressed a training-stability problem (router logits could grow unbounded and destabilize training, addressed with a router z-loss) and explored an entirely different paradigm — instead of each token picking its top-K experts, expert-choice routing lets each expert pick its top-tokens, which guarantees perfect load balance by construction but complicates causal / autoregressive generation.

**Mixtral (2024)** didn't introduce new MoE mechanics so much as prove the whole approach could be trained and released as a strong open-weight model — top-2-of-8 routing with a fairly standard softmax router — and normalized MoE as a mainstream, non-exotic architecture choice for anyone building an open model.

**DeepSeekMoE (2024)** is the actual fork point for almost everything covered below. Its two contributions were, first, fine-grained expert segmentation — instead of a handful of large experts, use many small ones, which gives the router far more combinatorial flexibility in how it allocates capacity to a token — and second, isolating one or more shared experts that process every token unconditionally, so that common, general-purpose computation doesn't have to be redundantly re-learned across dozens of routed experts. Four of the five reports covered here cite this paper directly or build on a lineage that does.

**DeepSeek-V3 (late 2024)** then made three changes that turned the DeepSeekMoE recipe into what's now closer to an industry default: sigmoid gating instead of softmax, an auxiliary-loss-free load balancing method (a learned per-expert bias term added to routing scores and adjusted up or down based on recent load, rather than a separate loss term competing with the language-modeling objective), and a multi-token prediction (MTP) module trained alongside the main model. This exact combination — fine-grained routed experts, one shared expert, sigmoid gating, bias-based balancing — is what GLM-5.2 and DeepSeek-V4 both still use, almost unchanged, in 2026.

Two more recent, narrower ideas matter for what follows without being direct ancestors of the DeepSeekMoE line: **Hash Layers (Roller et al., 2021)** showed you could route tokens with a fixed, deterministic hash function instead of a learned gate at all, trading routing quality for guaranteed-even load and zero routing-network overhead — an idea revived for a narrow purpose in DeepSeek-V4. And **Mixture-of-Depths (2024)**, which let transformers route tokens through or around entire compute blocks based on a learned per-token decision, is the clearest conceptual ancestor of the "let some tokens skip real computation" idea that shows up in LongCat-2.0, alongside a separate 2024 technique for accelerating expert-parallel communication, ScMoE, that LongCat's backbone is built on directly.

## 3. DeepSeek-V4: the direct heir, refining rather than replacing

[DeepSeek-V4](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2606.19348) ships two models, DeepSeek-V4-Flash (284B total, 13B active) and DeepSeek-V4-Pro (1.6T total, 49B active), and both keep the DeepSeekMoE fine-grained-plus-shared-expert framework essentially intact: 256 routed experts and 1 shared expert for Flash, 384 routed experts and 1 shared expert for Pro, top-6 for both. The paper is explicit that these are "minor adjustments" from V3, and the adjustments are genuinely incremental rather than structural: the affinity-scoring function changes from V3's sigmoid to Sqrt(Softplus), and the aux-loss-free bias-based balancing mechanism gets a small additional sequence-wise balance loss layered on top, specifically to stop imbalance within a single long sequence (relevant given V4's million-token context target) even when balance looks fine averaged across a whole training batch.

The more interesting changes are two narrower interventions. First, the first three MoE layers switch from a learned router to Hash routing — deterministic, no gradient, no possibility of collapse — a direct revival of Roller et al.'s 2021 idea, used here specifically where DeepSeek-V3 used to have dense (non-MoE) layers. Second, a training-stability technique called Anticipatory Routing: when the model detects a loss spike (which the paper ties specifically to outlier activations inside MoE layers), it starts computing routing decisions from a slightly stale copy of the router's own weights rather than the current ones, decoupling how fast the router changes from how fast the rest of the model changes, until the instability passes. On the engineering side, DeepSeek-V4 also introduces a new pipelined expert-parallelism kernel (open-sourced as part of DeepGEMM under the name MegaMoE) that overlaps the dispatch, compute, and combine stages of expert-parallel MoE across "waves" of experts, reporting meaningful speedups over a comparable overlap scheme called Comet.

## 4. GLM-5.2: the recipe as industry default

[GLM-5.2](https://www.zhipuai.cn/zh/research/161) is, per third-party technical coverage, a 753B-total / 40B-active model using 256 routed experts, top-8, plus 1 shared expert, with sigmoid gating and DeepSeekMoE-style aux-loss-free balancing — which is to say, essentially the same MoE recipe as DeepSeek-V3, largely unchanged. That's not a criticism; it's the more interesting observation. Zhipu's own release material barely discusses MoE architecture at all, instead focusing on IndexShare (an attention-side optimization that shares one sparse-attention indexer across every four layers, cutting per-token FLOPs at long context by roughly a factor of 2.9), an improved multi-token-prediction layer for speculative decoding (raising accepted-token length by up to 20%), and Slime, an in-house framework for large-scale agentic reinforcement learning. By 2026, the fine-grained-experts-plus-shared-expert-plus-sigmoid-plus-aux-loss-free combination has become stable enough, and well-understood enough, that a frontier lab can treat it as a solved layer and spend its differentiation budget entirely elsewhere.

## 5. MAI-Thinking-1: adopting LatentMoE, and dropping the shared expert

Microsoft AI's [MAI-Thinking-1](https://microsoft.ai/pdf/mai-thinking-1.pdf) (34.7B active / 962B total, 78 layers) is the first of the five to genuinely restructure the MoE layer itself rather than tune its balancing mechanics. It adopts LatentMoE — an architecture first proposed by NVIDIA in early 2026 and already shipping in NVIDIA's own Nemotron-3 Super and Ultra models — which compresses each token into a smaller latent dimension before the expert computation and all-to-all communication happen, then expands back afterward. Because both the per-expert compute and the network traffic shrink by the same factor as the compression, the saved budget gets reinvested in more experts and a larger top-K: MAI-Thinking-1 runs 512 experts at top-8, having grown from an earlier 192-expert / top-4 configuration specifically once LatentMoE made the larger configuration affordable to communicate.

Three of MAI-Thinking-1's other choices are worth noting because they cut against the DeepSeek-V3 default. It uses ordinary softmax gating, computed on the token's original uncompressed representation even though the actual expert math happens in the compressed latent space. Its load-balancing loss is a fairly traditional GShard-style auxiliary loss rather than a bias-based free-balancing term — but the paper reports that once the loss is aggregated globally across the whole training batch rather than per-microbatch, it performs about as well as the bias-based approach, suggesting the aggregation strategy matters more than which specific balancing formula is used. And, after testing shared experts, the team found they only helped in architectures that run MoE at every layer; MAI-Thinking-1 interleaves dense feed-forward layers with sparse MoE layers rather than making every layer sparse, and in that interleaved layout a shared expert added no measurable benefit — so the shipped model doesn't have one. The model also runs fully dropless (no token-dropping capacity limit at all), a choice the paper says was driven by capacity-related inconsistencies that showed up in their balancing ablations once a hard cap was in the picture.

## 6. Kimi K3: pushing granularity to an extreme, and replacing the balancing term

[Kimi K3](https://www.kimi.com/blog/kimi-k3) is the largest model in this comparison at 2.8 trillion total parameters (Moonshot has not disclosed an active-parameter figure), and it pushes expert granularity further than any other 2026 report: 896 experts with top-16 selected per token, inside a framework Moonshot calls Stable LatentMoE — evidently the same latent-space-compression family as MAI-Thinking-1's LatentMoE, applied at a more extreme granularity. The release blog confirms a shared-expert component exists in the architecture (visible in its own architecture diagram) without disclosing how many.

The more novel piece is how Kimi K3 handles load balancing. Rather than DeepSeek-V3's learned bias term, it uses what Moonshot calls Quantile Balancing: expert allocation is derived directly from the quantiles of the router's own score distribution, which the release material describes as eliminating both the heuristic bias-update rule and a load-balancing hyperparameter that needed separate tuning. At 16-of-896 sparsity, small routing imperfections have more room to compound than they do at DeepSeek-V3's 8-of-256, so it's a reasonable place for a frontier lab to want one less hyperparameter to get wrong. Kimi K3 also introduces Per-Head Muon (extending the Muon optimizer to update attention heads independently) and reports training the model with quantization-aware training from the supervised fine-tuning stage onward, using MXFP4 weights with MXFP8 activations.

## 7. LongCat-2.0: the one that changes the shape of the pool itself

[LongCat-2.0](https://longcat.chat/blog/longcat-2.0/) (1.6T total, averaging roughly 48B active — reported as ranging 33B to 56B depending on the token) is structurally the odd one out. Every other model in this post keeps some version of "N routed experts, pick top-K" as a fixed shape; LongCat changes the shape per token. Its expert pool includes what Meituan calls Zero-Computation Experts: experts that do no computation at all and simply return the input unchanged. The router decides, per token, how many real experts versus zero-computation experts to use — a simple token like punctuation might route almost entirely to zero-computation experts and cost next to nothing, while a difficult token engages more real expert capacity. This is a token-adaptive compute budget rather than a fixed top-K, conceptually closer to Mixture-of-Depths' idea of letting tokens skip computation than to anything in the DeepSeekMoE lineage.

On top of that, LongCat's MoE backbone uses a shortcut-connected design (ScMoE) specifically built to improve expert-parallel throughput, and the routed experts themselves are split into three task-specialized groups — Agent, Reasoning, and Interaction — with routing gated per task rather than treating the whole pool as one undifferentiated set of interchangeable specialists. A separate 135-billion-parameter n-gram embedding module sits alongside the MoE stack entirely. Meituan has not published detailed load-balancing mechanics for this design, which is a real gap in an otherwise unusually well-documented release — the zero-computation-expert mechanism in particular raises an obvious balancing question (what stops the router from routing everything to zero-computation experts to minimize loss on easy tokens at training time) that the public material doesn't yet answer.

## 9. What's converged, and what's still an open argument

Fine-grained routed experts plus one isolated shared expert is no longer a design choice being actively debated — it's the substrate every model in this post starts from, DeepSeekMoE's 2024 contribution having become table stakes within about two years. Sigmoid-family gating (or a close variant like Sqrt(Softplus)) has similarly displaced softmax across every model here except MAI-Thinking-1, which is the one architecture that also runs MoE at less than every layer, suggesting the gating-function choice may be somewhat coupled to how densely MoE is applied through the network rather than a universal improvement.

Load balancing is the part still visibly being reinvented every few months: a learned per-expert bias term (DeepSeek-V3, GLM-5.2), that same bias term plus an added sequence-level loss (DeepSeek-V4), a fully global-batch aggregated traditional auxiliary loss (MAI-Thinking-1), and quantile-derived allocation with no separate hyperparameter at all (Kimi K3) are four different answers shipping in the same summer. None of the released material makes a clean, controlled case that any one of these dominates the others — mostly because each was tuned inside a specific architecture (different granularity, different gating function, different degree of dropless-ness) that makes an apples-to-apples comparison hard to extract from public reports alone.

The most consequential open fork is between two different ways of widening the gap between total and active parameters. LatentMoE (MAI-Thinking-1, Kimi K3) keeps a static top-K but shrinks the effective cost of a larger K and larger N by moving the computation into a compressed latent space — more experts, more of them active, but each one cheaper. LongCat-2.0's zero-computation experts instead keep the pool's nominal size but make the actual number of "real" experts activated a dynamic, token-dependent quantity — same idea in spirit as Mixture-of-Depths, applied inside the expert dimension rather than the depth dimension. These are not mutually exclusive, and it would not be surprising to see a 2027-era report combine both: latent-space compression to make a bigger pool affordable, and adaptive per-token activation to avoid spending that budget uniformly on tokens that don't need it.

## 10. How to read an MoE spec sheet

**Active parameters, not total parameters, predict inference cost per token** — a 2.8T-parameter model with a small top-K can be cheaper to serve than a 300B dense model, and total parameter counts are usually the headline precisely because they're the more impressive-sounding number, not the more operationally relevant one.

**A shared expert is not a routed expert that always wins the router's vote** — it's architecturally separate, with its own weights, that never participates in the top-K competition at all. Its presence or absence is itself a design choice, not a detail: MAI-Thinking-1 shipping without one, specifically because its interleaved dense/sparse layout made a shared expert redundant, is a reminder that "the standard recipe" is a default, not a law.

**Bigger expert pools cost more in communication, not just in weight storage** — every routed token has to physically travel, over an interconnect, to whichever accelerator holds its selected expert (the [distributed-training post](#/blog?id=distributed-training-for-dl) covers this all-to-all pattern in more depth). Hash routing's revival in DeepSeek-V4 and LatentMoE's compressed communication payload are both, from different angles, responses to this same cost, which only gets more pressing as expert counts climb into the hundreds.

**Treat undisclosed numbers as undisclosed, not as zero or as embarrassing gaps** — Kimi K3's blog post is genuinely silent on active-parameter count and exact shared-expert count, and LongCat-2.0's balancing mechanics for its zero-computation experts aren't public yet. Frontier labs disclose what's useful to their positioning and no more; a missing number is a fact about the disclosure, not necessarily a fact about the architecture.

*The technical claims in this post are drawn directly from each model's own release material where fetched in full (Kimi K3, MAI-Thinking-1, DeepSeek-V4), and from third-party technical coverage where the primary source didn't include architecture-level MoE detail (GLM-5.2, LongCat-2.0) — noted inline above. As with any comparison across simultaneously-released reports, some of what reads as a stable convergence here may look different in six months.*
